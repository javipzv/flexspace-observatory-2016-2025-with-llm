Section 1

How does the time of a booking affect the length of meetings or the size of the booked resource?

The relationship between booking times and meeting lengths illustrates significant trends across the markets of Spain, the United Kingdom, and the United States. Each region exhibits unique patterns in resource demand, shaped by local preferences for timing and group sizes. For example, while early mornings see peak booking durations, there is a notable decline throughout the day, particularly for smaller groups, revealing how meeting requirements vary depending on timing and organizational dynamics.

Moreover, the analysis of resource capacity across the three countries further highlights the impact of group sizes on booking behaviors. Larger groups appear to maintain longer meeting durations and more stable demand throughout the day, contrasting sharply with the sharp drops seen in smaller teams. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for businesses aiming to optimize resource allocation and enhance scheduling efficiency in alignment with their operational needs.

Key findings

Morning Meetings Peak in All Markets

Bookings reach their highest averages with Spain at 213 minutes and the UK at 255 minutes during morning hours, highlighting consistent peak demand early in the day.

Large Groups Favor Longer Meetings

In the UK, larger groups of 101-250 members experience meeting durations peaking at approximately 290 minutes, demonstrating their preference for extended discussions compared to smaller teams.

Evening Resource Capacity Peaks

Resource capacity peaks at 7.36 in the US by 18H, indicating a significant demand during after-office hours, particularly for larger bookings.

Mean duration in minutes by location

The analysis of booking durations across three countries reveals distinctive patterns influenced by local timing preferences and resource demand. In Spain, bookings peak at an early morning average of approximately 213 minutes, tapering significantly to around 73 minutes by the evening. In contrast, the United Kingdom experiences a more robust morning peak reaching about 255 minutes at 9 AM, with durations dropping to roughly 83 minutes by mid-afternoon. The United States offers a balanced approach, maintaining average booking lengths over 200 minutes in morning hours but showing a gentler decline throughout the day, settling around 106 minutes by 9 PM.

When examining booking durations by group size, smaller teams (0-40 members) exhibit dramatic declines post-morning, while larger groups (101-250 and above) maintain relatively higher average durations, with peaks around 290 minutes in the UK. Notably, larger groups also display a consistent pattern of longer meetings later in the day compared to their smaller counterparts, suggesting a trend where as group size increases, bookings tend to extend over longer durations, highlighting differing operational needs.

Mean resource capacity by booking hour and location

The analysis reveals distinct patterns in resource capacity across Spain, the UK, and the US, influenced by both booking hours and group sizes. In Spain, resource capacity peaks in the evening, reaching up to 6.90 late in the day, while the UK experiences an early evening peak at 7.11, after which there is a significant drop. The US market aligns with the UK, witnessing a steady rise to 7.36 by 18H, though it starts from a notably lower capacity. Overall, aggregated data shows a cross-country peak of 6.50 at 18H, but declines significantly afterwards, indicating high demand during peak business hours.

Group size also impacts booking behavior; smaller groups (0-40 members) exhibit sharp peaks in the evening, while larger groups show more stable demand throughout the day. For instance, the 41-100 members category maintains a consistent capacity, peaking at 7.02 at 19H, while 101-250 members show a preference for more flexible scheduling, contributing to less fluctuation in demand. Overall, the findings suggest operational strategies need to adapt to these variances in resource utilization to better cater to the unique demands of various group sizes and time slots.

Section 2

How does the capacity of a resource affect the number of bookings it receives?

The analysis of resource capacity and its correlation to booking frequency reveals significant trends across various markets, emphasizing a clear preference for smaller resources. In both the US and UK, lower capacity categories dominate bookings, with a marked decrease in demand as resource size increases. This phenomenon suggests an overarching market trend: customers gravitating towards more intimate, manageable spaces rather than larger options, which see minimal interest.

Additionally, while higher capacity resources occasionally experience a surge in bookings under specific conditions, the general inclination towards smaller capacities indicates a potential opportunity for operators to refine their offerings. This consistent trend across markets signals an important consideration for resource management, as operators may benefit from focusing on smaller to mid-sized resources in response to user preferences.

Key findings

Preference for Smaller Capacities

Across markets, lower capacity resources dominate bookings, with the US seeing 23% of demand for 1-3 capacity resources.

Steep Declines in Larger Capacities

Demand for resources above 10 capacity sharply drops, with categories like 18-19 capturing less than 1% of total bookings.

Niche Demand for Extra-Large Spaces

In Spain, larger capacities (>21) demonstrate sporadic increases, indicating a specialized but present demand among users.

Booking distribution by capacity

Analysis of the booking distribution by capacity reveals a pronounced preference for lower capacity resources across various markets. In the US, the 1-3 capacity bin captures the highest percentage of bookings at approximately 23%, with a noticeable decline as capacity increases. The trend remains consistent, highlighting that resources with capacity beyond 10 show sharp drops in demand, with some categories like 18-19 seeing less than 1% of total bookings. Similarly, the UK market echoes this trend, peaking at 20% for the 4-5 capacity bin while larger capacities suffer steep declines.

In Spain, the most desired capacity is the 4-5 bin, attracting nearly 27% of bookings, although similar declines reflect user preferences for smaller spaces. Each membership category surveyed shows clearer demand toward smaller resource capacities. However, larger capacities receive sporadic increases for the >21 bin, indicating niche demand. Across the board, the market demonstrates an unequivocal inclination toward smaller spaces, with significant occupancy drops as resource capacities rise, suggesting potential opportunities for operators to focus on optimizing smaller to mid-sized space offerings.

Section 3

What is the mean price of a booking depending on the type of user that is using the resource?

This section delves into the mean pricing of bookings based on user types across major markets, providing insights into how demand fluctuates depending on customer segmentation. The analysis reveals notable trends in Spain, the UK, and the US, highlighting different market dynamics that shape user experiences and pricing strategies.

We explore how membership status significantly influences booking prices, with members consistently enjoying lower rates compared to non-members. Additionally, the examination of pricing variations by plan category showcases a clear market segmentation that reflects user expectations and service levels, setting the stage for an in-depth look at the data across these diverse markets.

Key findings

Growing Trend in Spain's Booking Prices

Spain's mean final hourly booking prices increased substantially from €18.75 to nearly €26, particularly surging during summer months.

Membership Discounts Drive Price Variance

In the U.S., members enjoy mean booking prices that are approximately 50% lower, ranging from $45 to $59, compared to non-members at $69 to $82.

High Demand for Dedicated Workspaces

In the UK, office spaces command the highest average prices, peaking at £37.45, indicating strong demand for private work environments.

Mean Final hourly price

The analysis of the mean final hourly price across Spain, the United Kingdom, and the United States reveals distinct pricing trends influenced by varying market conditions. In Spain, prices rose significantly from 18.75 to nearly 26 over the analyzed period, particularly during summer months, indicating a steady increase in resource demand. Conversely, the UK experienced high volatility in its pricing, starting at 40.80, dipping to around 25, and peaking around mid-2020 before stabilizing in the mid-30s by mid-2024, reflecting a more erratic market response.

The US market, meanwhile, showcased a more stable upward trajectory, with prices beginning at approximately 33 and peaking at 41.85, similar to the UK, but quickly returning to a relatively stable range. Overall, while all regions depicted increasing trends, Spain displayed consistent growth, the UK showed significant fluctuations, and the US maintained stability with gradual rises, underscoring diverse dynamics and responses to demand shifts across the markets.

Mean price by customer type

The analysis of mean booking prices based on customer types reveals a consistent trend across various markets. In the U.S., members benefit from lower prices, averaging around 50% less than non-members. For instance, members' mean prices range from approximately $45 to $59 compared to $69 to $82 for non-members, with disparities of about $22 to $34 depending on coworking size. This indicates that membership significantly impacts pricing strategy, promoting customer loyalty and incentivizing non-members to convert to members.

Similarly, in the UK market, members enjoy mean final prices around £73, while non-members pay about £85, highlighting an £11 difference that supports membership benefits. In Spain, the disparity mirrors this trend, with members’ prices roughly 33% lower than non-members, which incentivizes membership through substantial savings. Overall, the findings demonstrate how differentiated pricing strategies effectively enhance membership uptake across various customer segments and coworking sizes.

Mean hourly price by plan category

The analysis of mean hourly prices by plan category reveals significant variances across different workspace offerings. In the US market, dedicated desks and office spaces command the highest average prices, which can be around 32-36% above the budget-friendly virtual options. For instance, the dedicated desk prices range on average from $29.63 to $37.86 depending on coworking size, while virtual workspace prices remain consistently lower by approximately 39-43% across all categories analyzed.

In the UK, similar trends are observed where office spaces lead with mean prices peaking at £37.45, indicating high demand for private work environments. The dedicated desk shows fluctuating prices around £31-32, while virtual plans remain at the lower end, often below £25. In Spain, premium offerings such as the office and other specialized categories remain higher priced by as much as 30% compared to dedicated desks, with hot desks and virtual options providing more affordable solutions, targeting a different segment of users. This encapsulates a clear market segmentation where price adjustments reflect service levels and user expectations across varying coworking environments.

Conclusion

Smaller is better!

The analysis across various markets reveals distinct patterns in resource demand that underscore not only preferences for meeting times and sizes but also emphasize the implications of pricing structures. Morning meetings consistently peak in all regions, with the UK and Spain reporting exceptionally high durations, highlighting a universal trend towards early collaboration. This is further complicated by the noticeable demand from larger groups who prefer extended engagements, particularly in the UK, suggesting that businesses are valuing thorough discussions that go beyond the typical meeting duration. However, the pronounced decline in bookings for resources accommodating larger capacities further illustrates a clear market segmentation, indicating that while there is a specialized demand for extra-large spaces in places like Spain, the majority of bookings lean towards smaller capacity resources, particularly in the US, where the overwhelming preference for 1-3 capacity significantly shapes the landscape of resource demand.

Moreover, regional differences in pricing strategies reveal insights into market dynamics and consumer behavior. The substantial increase in mean booking prices in Spain, particularly during peak seasons, suggests a growing market demand, which providers can leverage sustainably. Contrasting this trend is the pricing differential facilitated by membership in the U.S. market, where members access significantly lower rates, revealing an avenue for businesses to drive customer loyalty and optimize revenue. Ultimately, the high demand for dedicated workspaces in markets such as the UK, coupled with the effective pricing strategies observed, not only reflects the intricate nature of resource allocation across markets but also underscores the industry's capacity to adapt and cater to evolving client needs.
