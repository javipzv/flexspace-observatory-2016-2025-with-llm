Section 1

How does the type of plan affect the permanence of members?

The examination of member tenure patterns within various membership plans reveals crucial insights into how the type of plan influences member commitment and retention. Notably, members enrolled in virtual and office plans exhibit significantly longer tenure compared to those utilizing more flexible arrangements like hot desks and part-time memberships. This suggests that structured plans foster a greater sense of stability and dedication among members, while more transient options often lead to higher turnover rates.

Furthermore, the analysis indicates a broader trend of increasing retention across all membership types from 2016 to 2025, particularly following the disruptions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic. The contrasting peaks in tenure for different plans during this period highlight an evolving landscape in workspace preferences, reflecting a stronger inclination towards long-term engagements as workplaces adapt to new norms.

Key findings

Longer Tenure with Formal Plans

Members with a 'Virtual' membership have an average tenure of 13.4 months, indicating a strong commitment compared to just 6.7 months for 'Hot Desk' users.

Post-Pandemic Membership Growth

Mean tenure for flexible workspace memberships surged from 5.73 months in 2016 to over 14 months in 2023, reflecting increased retention after the COVID-19 pandemic.

Stable Membership Preference

Dedicated Desk and Virtual plans experienced peak engagement around 2023, showcasing a heightened demand for stability in work environments.

How does the type of plan affect the permanence of members?

The analysis of member tenure by type of membership plan reveals significant variations in engagement duration. The highest average tenure is observed in the "Virtual" membership at approximately 13.4 months, closely followed by the "Office" membership with an average of about 11.9 months. These results indicate that members with more formalized plans exhibit a stronger commitment to their memberships, signaling a clear preference for stability in their work environments. Conversely, the "Hot Desk" membership category reflects the lowest engagement, with an average tenure of only 6.7 months, suggesting a tendency towards less commitment and higher turnover among these members.

Additionally, the "Part-time" membership shows a tenure of around 7.1 months, which is slightly better than hot desks but still below the dedicated options. The overall trends suggest a clear correlation between the level of commitment inherent in each membership type and the average duration of member engagement, with more structured plans encouraging longer-term relationships with the organization.

Evolution &user behaviour in the flexible workspace industry

The analysis of member tenure patterns in the flexible workspace industry from 2016 to 2025 reveals an overall growth in retention, with mean tenure increasing significantly from about 5.73 months in early 2016 to peaks above 14 months in 2023. The effects of the COVID-19 pandemic are particularly evident, as tenure experienced both a notable downturn and subsequent recovery, highlighting a shift towards longer commitments in the workplace post-pandemic.

Membership types show varied trends, with Dedicated Desk and Virtual plans demonstrating the highest peaks around 2023, indicating a heightened demand during that period. Conversely, plans like Hot Desk and Part-time maintained steadier growth without dramatic volatility. This suggests that, across all membership types, there has been a collective rebound in the flexible workspace market post-COVID, reflecting an evolving landscape shaped by changing work preferences.

Section 2

How does the type of plan affect the permanence of members?

Understanding how user profiles impact member tenure is crucial for enhancing retention strategies within the organization. Our analysis reveals significant trends associated with age and membership types, demonstrating that older members tend to stay longer, particularly in dedicated office environments, while virtual arrangements show even greater average tenures for older demographics. Conversely, part-time memberships struggle with lower engagement, highlighting an area needing targeted improvement.

Additionally, distinguishing between company freelancers and team members unveils subtle differences in commitment levels, affecting overall retention rates. The data shows that team members, integrated within collective dynamics, generally enjoy longer tenures than freelancers. This nuanced perspective provides valuable insights into the varying experiences of members based on their work environments and profiles, setting the stage for more detailed exploration of these trends.

Key findings

Age and Membership Type Impact Tenure

Older members in Virtual memberships exhibit the highest mean tenure at 15.7 months, contrasting sharply with the significantly lower tenure of Part-time memberships.

Team Members Enjoy Longer Commitment

Team members have an average tenure of 10.31 months, 2% longer than freelancers, suggesting stronger integration leads to prolonged engagement.

Office Memberships Favor Longevity

Office memberships have a mean tenure of 12.17 months for members, highlighting a strong preference for long-term commitment as age increases.

How does the age of members affect their tenure?

The analysis indicates a clear relationship between age and member tenure across various membership types. For Dedicated Desk members, mean tenure rises steadily from around 5.7 months for younger users to approximately 9 months for those aged 45-54, with a slight dip in the oldest age group. Hot Desk and Office memberships exhibit similar trends, with tenure peaking at around 10 months for older members, particularly Office memberships, which showcase the highest overall mean tenure values. In contrast, Virtual memberships reveal exceptional engagement, with mean tenure soaring to about 15.7 months for the oldest age group, reflecting the strong appeal of remote work among older demographics.

Part-time memberships lag significantly, presenting the lowest mean tenure figures across the board, particularly among older members, indicating potential retention challenges. The Office membership category consistently demonstrates a preference for long-term commitment as members age, while the variability seen in Part-time memberships signifies an area for growth. Overall, these findings underscore the importance of both membership type and age in shaping engagement and retention strategies within the organization.

How does tenure compare between freelancers and teams?

The analysis of member tenure patterns reveals that freelancers, categorized as 'Company' members, have an average tenure of approximately 10.1 months, slightly shorter than the 10.31 months observed for team members, or 'Members'. This marginal difference of about 2% indicates that team members are more likely to remain with the organization longer, possibly reflecting stronger integration into the team dynamics.

Looking at specific working environments, the tenure varies widely across categories. For instance, dedicated desk setups show close averages of 8.54 months for companies and 8.07 months for members, while office environments significantly favor members with an average tenure of 12.17 months compared to 9.61 months for companies. Notably, virtual arrangements yield the highest tenures, with averages around 13.5 months, suggesting that the flexibility of virtual setups fosters prolonged commitments from both freelancers and teams.

Section 3

New Charts

The flexible workspace industry has witnessed notable shifts in member tenure patterns, particularly in relation to coworking space size. Data indicates that smaller environments, catering to fewer members, tend to foster longer retention rates, suggesting a strong sense of community and engagement among users. Meanwhile, larger coworking spaces struggle with member retention as average tenure significantly declines across various membership types, highlighting potential challenges in maintaining user satisfaction at scale.

Moreover, an analysis of tenure trends over the years reveals distinct growth patterns, with smaller spaces showing remarkable increases in average length of stay compared to their larger counterparts. These findings emphasize that the dynamics of member retention are closely tied to workspace size, particularly as demographic factors also play a vital role in influencing tenure across different age groups. Overall, the insights gathered lay the groundwork for understanding how space size impacts member behavior in the evolving coworking landscape.

Key findings

Size Matters: Tenure Trends in Coworking

In coworking spaces with 0-40 members, average member tenure peaks at nearly 21 months, while those in spaces with over 250 members plummet to just 5.7 months.

Age Impacts Member Retention

Members aged 45-54 in smaller coworking spaces maintain tenures averaging nearly 14 years, contrasting sharply with just 8.5 years for the 17-24 age group in larger environments.

Declining Stays in Larger Settings

Individual members in spaces with over 250 members experience average tenures of only 8 months, compared to around 12 months in smaller coworking environments.

Mean tenure by membership type

The analysis of mean tenure by membership type reveals a clear downward trend in member retention as coworking space size increases. In environments with 0-40 members, virtual memberships boast the longest average tenure at nearly 16 months, significantly outperforming dedicated desks and part-time memberships. As coworking sizes scale to 41-100 members, average tenures decline across the board, with hot desk memberships experiencing about a 10% drop, while virtual memberships remain relatively stable at approximately 14.5 months.

In spaces housing over 250 members, the average tenure plummets to as low as 5.7 months for hot desks, showcasing significant disengagement. Notably, both dedicated desks and part-time memberships also see considerable declines. The data clearly indicates that smaller coworking environments foster stronger retention, particularly for virtual and office memberships, while larger spaces negatively impact member engagement and satisfaction.

Evolution of tenure

The analysis of member tenure patterns across coworking space sizes from 2016 to 2025 reveals significant trends in average tenure growth. Smaller spaces, accommodating up to 40 members, exhibited an impressive increase in average tenure from approximately 7.79 months in early 2016 to a peak of around 21 months by early 2023, followed by slight stabilization around 14 months in mid-2023. In contrast, spaces with 41-100 members showed a steady growth from 7.11 months in 2016 to about 19.63 months in 2023, with fluctuations suggesting varying user engagement influenced by market conditions.

On the other hand, the series for larger coworking spaces, specifically those with 101-250 and above 250 members, displayed slower average tenure growth rates, peaking at approximately 17.57 months and 17.47 months respectively. These groups showcased a more stable membership retention profile but less socio-temporal volatility, indicating a potential correlation between space size and user behavior patterns. Overall, the data suggests that smaller coworking spaces tend to attract members with higher initial commitment but experience greater fluctuations, while larger spaces benefit from gradual stabilization in tenure.

Mean tenure by age

The analysis of mean tenure by age across different coworking space sizes reveals a clear trend: smaller coworking environments support longer member tenures. For instance, in spaces with 0-40 members, the average tenure peaks at nearly 14 years for the 45-54 age bracket, compared to significantly lower tenures of about 8.5 years for members aged 17-24. This pattern continues across larger coworking spaces, where average tenures decrease overall, suggesting that larger environments may struggle more with retaining members, particularly the younger demographic.

Moreover, as age increases, tenure tends to rise, though this effect diminishes for spaces with over 250 members, where the average tenure remains comparatively low across age groups. The decline in tenure post-54 years is notably pronounced in the largest coworking segments, highlighting a distinct challenge in engagement strategies for older members in these settings. Overall, the findings emphasize the importance of workspace size on member retention rates, with smaller spaces fostering longer tenures regardless of age.

Average length of stay by coworking size

The analysis of average length of stay in coworking spaces reveals a clear decline in tenancy with increasing workspace size. For coworking spaces accommodating 0-40 members, both individual members and companies exhibit the longest stays, averaging around 12 months and above. However, as the size expands to the next categories, average stays drop significantly; companies in the largest coworking size (+250 members) have an average tenure of only about 8 months, a stark contrast to the roughly 12 months observed in the smallest spaces.

Overall, the trend indicates that both member types experience reduced tenure as the coworking environment grows. The gap in average stay between companies and individuals widens in larger spaces, suggesting individual members may find a stronger community and more effective retention strategies in smaller settings. This reinforces the notion that larger coworking spaces may struggle with tenant retention compared to their smaller counterparts.

Conclusions

If you want to improve your retention values you should concentrate on getting members on virtual and office plans. However, if your priority is members on Part-time, Dedicated Desk and Hot Desk rates, team members will offer you up to 16% longer tenure

Globally, we have found that concentrating on attracting members aged 45+ will give you at least 20% more tenure than any other age group.

Looking to the future, taking into account how the sector has evolved in recent years plus the impact of the pandemic which has accelerated the entry of new customers into the sector from a traditional model who have decided to migrate in search of flexibility at a time when uncertainty has increased, we believe that permanence will continue to increase unless some unexpected external factor impacts the industry globally.
