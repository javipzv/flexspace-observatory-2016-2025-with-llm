How has the ratio of members per resource evolved?

The analysis of the ratio of members per resource in coworking spaces reveals significant shifts from early 2016 to 2025, highlighting a general decline across various space sizes. Smaller facilities have struggled with occupancy, while mid-sized and larger options have also witnessed sharp decreases, reflecting the growing challenges of maintaining demand amidst changing economic landscapes.

A closer look at different countries shows contrasting trends in resource utilization. While Spain and the UK have experienced notable declines since recent peaks, the United States has demonstrated relative stability despite minor fluctuations. These patterns indicate that coworking resources are increasingly under pressure, with the effects varying substantially by market, setting the stage for a deeper exploration of these trends.

Key findings

Diminishing Ratios in Smaller Spaces

The mean members per resource for smaller coworking spaces dropped significantly from an initial high to just 2.60 by 2023.

Steep Decline in Mid-Sized Spaces

Mid-sized coworking spaces saw their average members per resource fall sharply from 17.08 in 2016 to 8.37 by June 2024.

Stability Amid Fluctuations in the US

Unlike other countries, the US maintained an average of around 10 members per resource, despite a notable dip during mid-2020.

Mean members per resource by location

The analysis of the mean members per resource in coworking spaces from January 2016 to April 2025 illustrates a declining trend across different size categories. Smaller spaces (0-40 Members) began with a strong ratio but faced a significant decrease, culminating at a low of 2.60 in 2023, reflecting a challenge in maintaining occupancy. Meanwhile, mid-sized coworking spaces (41-100 Members) started with an average of 17.08 and decreased sharply to approximately 8.37 by June 2024, revealing a concerning drop post-2021. Larger spaces (+250 Members), while initially showing high ratios, witnessed a dramatic fall, reaching 23.45 by December 2024 due to the downturn in demand, illustrating vulnerabilities during economic shifts.

Examining specific countries highlights varying demand dynamics. In Spain, there was notable fluctuation with a peak usage of 11.06 in 2018, followed by a steady decline to 7.7 in 2023. The United States, in contrast, maintained relative stability throughout the period, with minimal fluctuations around 10 members per resource but did face a noticeable dip during mid-2020. Overall, these trends suggest that coworking resources are under increasing strain, with varied impacts observed across different markets.

Conclusion

The future is flex

The global analysis of resource demand within the coworking space sector reveals significant trends that highlight the evolving landscape of this industry. Notably, smaller coworking spaces have faced a substantial decline in members per resource, dropping to an average of 2.60 by 2023, while mid-sized venues experienced a steep reduction from 17.08 in 2016 to just 8.37 by mid-2024. In contrast, larger coworking spaces with over 250 members have witnessed a notable resurgence in booking activity, indicating a strong recovery in demand as organizations adapt to post-pandemic workplace needs. This divergence reflects not only the shifting consumer preferences towards larger, more flexible environments but also the implications of geographical variances, with the UK leading the market in recovery and pricing trends.

Furthermore, the emergence of flexible bookings, particularly among small groups, underscores a broader shift towards adaptability in workspace usage, with booking durations increasing significantly in response to new workplace dynamics. Consistent booking patterns in major markets, particularly a preference for morning slots and midweek engagements, further illustrate the changing demands and behaviors of users. As the industry moves forward, the stability of larger coworking venues coupled with the volatility faced by smaller ones highlights the necessity for operators to rethink strategies, pricing structures, and resource allocation to meet the evolving preferences of today's workforce.
