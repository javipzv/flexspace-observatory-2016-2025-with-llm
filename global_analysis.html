Section 1

How has the ratio of members per resource evolved?

The intensity of resource use in coworking spaces climbed steadily through late 2018, as more members competed for each available workstation, meeting room, or locker. When the pandemic arrived in spring 2020, this ratio dropped sharply, halting a long period of sustained growth across all site sizes.

In the years that followed, recovery has been uneven: smaller venues now sit below their pre-2016 levels, larger operations have flattened out in the mid-20s, and the overall average remains shy of its late-2018 peak. Regionally, the United Kingdom consistently leads in members per resource, Spain trails, and the United States falls in between, yet all markets share a universal cycle of pre-pandemic highs, COVID-era dips, and gradual stabilization.

Key findings

Pandemic slump trims resource demand

The average members-per-resource ratio plunged by 20–30% during spring 2020 and has since only partially rebounded to about 10.5 by 2025.

Small sites fall below pre-2016 density

Smaller coworking spaces now have about 2.6 members per resource in 2025, down from 3.2 before 2016.

Uniform regional patterns reflect global cycle

All regions endured 15–30% drops in member-per-resource ratios during spring 2020 and have only seen modest recovery by 2025.

Mean members per resource by location

Across all coworking‐space sizes, the members-per-resource ratio climbed or held steady into late 2018—small sites peaking near 4.4, the largest spaces in the 30s, and the overall average hitting 11.4—before a COVID-driven drop of roughly 20–30% in spring 2020. Although there was a partial rebound afterwards, smaller tiers have since fallen below their pre-2016 levels (now about 2.6 versus 3.2), larger operations flattened out in the mid-20s, and the aggregate ratio has stabilized around 10.5 by spring 2025, still shy of its late-2018 highs. Regionally, the UK consistently leads with mid-teen ratios, Spain trails with peaks just above 11 and a current level near 9.6, and the United States sits in between around 10–11. All markets share a cycle of mid-2018 highs, sharp COVID-era dips (15–30%), and only modest recoveries by 2025, highlighting a universal pattern of growth, shock, and gradual stabilization.

Section 2

How has the number of bookings evolved?

Global bookings exhibited a clear seasonal beat before 2020—spring and autumn peaks with summer and winter lulls—and trended steadily upward until the pandemic triggered a dramatic collapse in early 2020. Since then, demand has rebounded strongly yet unevenly across regions and workspace sizes, with larger venues and certain markets recovering more vigorously while the familiar seasonal cycle endures.

At the same time, the balance of resources booked has been reshaped. Once dominated by meeting and conference rooms, the mix shifted sharply during lockdowns toward “Other” and flexible offerings, and although traditional formats have recovered somewhat, the higher baseline for agile options points to a more enduring shift in workspace preferences.

Key findings

COVID-induced booking collapse and surge

In April 2020, global bookings plunged by nearly 90% into the mid-teens before rebounding to peaks around 158 by March 2023.

Larger coworking spaces lead recovery

Post-pandemic, venues with over 250 members saw bookings exceed 410 compared to around 73 at the smallest sites.

Agile workspaces gain lasting share

Since 2020, hot desk bookings have climbed to about 13% of total while meeting rooms settled near 42%, signaling a shift to flexible formats.

Between 2016 and late 2019, bookings across coworking spaces and major markets displayed a clear seasonal rhythm—spring and autumn peaks with summer and winter troughs—lifting the global average from roughly 50 to about 110. The COVID shock in April 2020 triggered an 80–90% collapse across all size cohorts and regions, driving average bookings down into the mid-teens.

The post-pandemic rebound since late 2021 has been strong but varied: by March 2023 the combined global series and the US each peaked near 158. Largest sites (+250 members) soared above 410, midsize spaces reached around 190, and the smallest venues climbed to about 73. Regionally, the UK led with highs over 200, Spain neared 190, while the US showed milder swings. Common seasonality persists, yet scale and volatility now differ markedly by space size and market.

How types of resources have evolved from 2016 to 2021

Pre-2020, meeting rooms and conference rooms together accounted for over 80% of bookings—meeting rooms alone sitting near 60% and conference rooms around 20%—while flexible options like hot desks, booths and event spaces each held under 5%. The COVID lockdown in April 2020 reshuffled this balance: meeting rooms plunged to roughly 44% and conference rooms to about 8%, “Other” resources spiked above 40%, and hot desks and event spaces jumped into the mid-single digits.

In the ensuing years, traditional formats partially recovered but stabilized well below their former peaks (meeting rooms around 42%, conference rooms near 8%). Meanwhile, “Other” demand settled near 34% and flexible offerings retained much higher baselines than pre-pandemic—hot desks at about 12–13%, booths around 4% and event spaces under 1.2%—pointing to a durable shift toward more agile workspace use.

Section 3

Has COVID-19 been a turning point in resource consumption patterns?

Since early 2020, the pandemic has reshaped how people use bookable workspaces around the globe, driving a notable shift toward longer average booking durations. Prior to COVID-19, session lengths held fairly steady, but the arrival of lockdowns and new health protocols sparked a marked increase in resource usage across customer types and space sizes. This overall uplift suggests that the crisis served as a clear turning point, prompting users to reserve workspaces for extended periods.

Behind this global trend, regional patterns and customer segments reveal further nuances. While all markets experienced longer bookings, countries such as the United Kingdom saw the most pronounced spikes, and Spain displayed greater seasonal swings. Despite these differences, the sustained rise in average session times underscores a broader move toward deeper engagement with workspace resources—an evolution we explore in detail in the subsections that follow.

Key findings

Sustained 20–30% Booking Duration Increase

Since April 2020, average booking lengths jumped by about 20–30% (from ~130 to ~160 minutes) and remained elevated through early 2025.

Non-member Small-Space Sessions Top 200 Minutes

Bookings by non-members in the smallest coworking spaces rose into the 170–185 minute range in 2020 and exceeded 200 minutes by late 2023.

UK Leads, Spain Lags with Seasonal Swings

The UK peaked near 190 minutes at the COVID spike while Spain dipped to around 100 minutes each August, highlighting divergent regional patterns.

In April 2020, average booking lengths surged by roughly 20–30% across all space sizes and customer types, jumping from the pre-pandemic norm of 125–140 minutes to around 150–165 minutes. Non-member bookings and the smallest coworking spaces saw the steepest rise, climbing into the 170–185 minute range and eventually topping 200 minutes by late 2023. Larger venues and member bookings also rose steadily, stabilizing around 160–170 minutes through early 2025—some 20–40 minutes longer than before COVID-19.

Regionally, the UK recorded the highest peaks (nearly 190 minutes at its COVID spike), while Spain remained the lowest and most seasonal, dipping near 100 minutes each August. The US and the global aggregate sat between these extremes, moving from about 135 minutes in mid-2019 to end-of-2023 highs near 170 minutes. Despite ongoing seasonal swings, the overall pattern shows a sustained uplift in booking duration, indicating that COVID-19 marked a clear turning point toward longer resource usage worldwide.

Section 4

Booking patterns by country booking based on time of the day, day of the week and month.

Resource booking across global markets follows clear daily and weekly cycles, with demand peaking in the early morning hours and tapering off by evening. Midweek days consistently dominate booking volume, while weekends see only a small share of activity, albeit slightly more pronounced in some regions than others.

Seasonal patterns further shape demand, with a spring surge giving way to a pronounced summer lull before rebounding sharply into autumn. Regional nuances emerge in this annual rhythm: the United States maintains a more balanced distribution, the United Kingdom benefits from a distinct autumn uplift, and Spain experiences the steepest mid-year decline.

Key findings

Morning Booking Peak at 9 AM

Bookings surge at 9 AM, with the UK hitting about 20% of daily volume and the global average at 17%.

Midweek Demand Dominates Weekly Bookings

Tuesday through Thursday each capture roughly 22% of bookings, while weekends drop below 3% in most markets.

Spring Peak and Summer Slump

Annual bookings peak in March at around 10%, plunge to about 7% in August, and then rally back to near 9% by autumn.

Booking distribution by time of the day

Across all markets, bookings concentrate in the morning around 9 AM, with the UK registering the strongest peak at about 20 % of its daily volume, followed by the global aggregate at 17 %, Spain at 16 %, and the US at 13 %. After a modest late-morning plateau, volumes taper through the afternoon and drop to negligible levels by evening. Weekly demand also clusters mid-week: Tuesday through Thursday each account for roughly 22 % of weekly bookings in aggregate, while Friday falls to 14 % and weekend share shrinks below 3 %—though the US maintains a slightly higher weekend slice (around 4 % on Saturdays) than Europe.

Seasonally, bookings peak in March (about 10 % of annual volume) before easing into a mid-year lull. The global low-point occurs in August at roughly 7 %, but Spain’s summer trough is particularly stark (just over 3 %). From September onward, demand rebounds into a late-year upswing, with October–November shares returning to near-peak levels (around 9–10 %). Overall, the US distribution is the most even, whereas Spain shows the sharpest summer collapse and the UK features a distinct autumn boost.

Section 5

Evolution of the average price of booking.

From 2016 through early 2020, average hourly rates for booking coworking spaces climbed steadily across all size categories, with smaller venues showing the greatest ups and downs and larger sites and mid-sized hubs maintaining a price premium. A pronounced spring 2020 spike briefly disrupted the gradual rise, and since then regional markets – led by the UK at the high end, Spain at the lower, and the US in between – have converged around a tighter global range, smoothing out pre-pandemic volatility into 2025.

At the same time, the share of zero-price bookings has followed a clear seasonal rhythm and pandemic-driven peaks, rising sharply in April 2020 and again through late 2021 before settling back toward mid-teen levels by spring 2025. While larger venues experienced the most dramatic swings in free-booking rates, smaller spaces remained comparatively steady, highlighting how demand cycles and pricing strategies have adjusted in the face of changing global conditions.

Key findings

Pandemic-Driven Hourly Rate Surge Across Segments

Hourly rates spiked by about 20–40% in April 2020, with large venues hitting nearly $49 before settling into the low-$30s by 2025.

Global Price Convergence by 2025

After volatile swings, global rates narrowed into a $29–37 band by April 2025, down from a $19–49 range during 2016–20.

Zero-Price Bookings Peak and Decline

Zero-price bookings rose to roughly 27% at year-end 2021 then retreated to about 16% by spring 2025.

Mean final hourly price

Hourly rates across coworking segments rose gradually from 2016 into early 2020, punctuated by a sharp April 2020 spike of roughly 20–40 percent across all size bands. Small spaces (0–40 members) were the most volatile, dipping below $22 in late 2016 and mid-2020 before recovering to about $30 by 2025. Mid-sized venues (41–100) followed a similar path but stayed slightly higher overall, peaking near $35 in spring 2020 and ending around $31. Larger sites (101–250 and 250+ members) commanded the highest rates throughout, surging to nearly $49 in April 2020 and settling in the $30–42 range by April 2025 as demand stabilized.

Regionally, Spain consistently showed the lowest averages—starting near $19 in 2016 and holding around $25–26 by 2025—while the UK led with values around $40–42 early on, dipping in summer and rebounding above $36 by 2025. The US sat between these extremes, moving mostly in the low- to mid-30s and finishing near $36. The global aggregate smoothed these swings, climbing from about $30 in 2016 to a 36-ish peak at the pandemic and ending around $34 in April 2025. All markets shared winter highs, summer troughs, and the pronounced April 2020 surge, then converged into a tighter $29–37 band in recent years.

Percentage of zero final hourly price by space size

Zero-price bookings for the final hourly rate follow a clear cycle: lows in mid-2017 give way to a mid-2018 rebound, an early-2020 bump, and a peak in late-2021 (small spaces under 40 members hit about 24 %, midsized around 27 %, and the largest over 35 %). Since that high, all categories have eased, with small venues dropping to single digits (~8 %), midsized to mid-teens (~15 %), and large spaces stabilizing near 25 %. The overall average rose from the mid-teens in 2016 to about 27 % at year-end 2021 before retreating to roughly 16 % by spring 2025. The share of zero base hourly price remains far higher but exhibits the same broad swings. From generally 65 %–75 % in 2016–19, all cohorts spiked in April 2020 (peaking near 75 %, and almost 79 % for the biggest venues) then gradually declined. By April 2025, most categories hover in the low-60 % range, with larger spaces showing the widest swings (roughly 50 % to 79 %) and smaller ones holding steadier between 62 % and 73 %.

Conclusion

The future is flex

The global downturn in resource demand during spring 2020 saw the average members-per-resource ratio plunge by up to 30%, with smaller coworking spaces falling below pre-2016 density levels and only a partial rebound to around 10.5 members per resource by early 2025. Across regions, this uniform cycle gave way to a stratified recovery in which larger venues scaled beyond 410 bookings while smaller sites lingered near 73, highlighting the resilience of high-capacity spaces. Moreover, the pandemic accelerated a shift toward agile work formats, as evidenced by hot desk and meeting room bookings climbing to roughly 13% and 42% of total volume, respectively, and a sustained 20–30% increase in booking durations that underscores growing demand for flexible, longer sessions by both members and non-members.

Price dynamics mirrored this volatility; hourly rates spiked by 20–40% at the onset of COVID-19—peaking at nearly $49 in large venues—before converging into a narrower $29–37 range by 2025, while zero-price bookings peaked at 27% in late 2021 then receded to 16%. Temporal patterns likewise reinforced a return to routine, with daily peaks at 9 AM accounting for nearly a fifth of volume, midweek sessions dominating 60% of bookings, and a clear annual rhythm that peaks in spring and dips through summer. These trends collectively illustrate a flexible workspace market that has adapted to pandemic shocks with more durable, longer-duration engagements and pricing stability, even as regional and size-based disparities continue to define the contours of recovery.
